### What the Signal Tracker Actually Means

Quick framing note before the data: I am not going to publish a win/loss accuracy percentage at Week 3. Here's why.

The April signals I called are designed to resolve over the **full season** — May through August is when the luck normalizes and stats move toward true talent. Calling Week 3 the verdict window is like judging a marathon at mile 8.

What I *can* show you is the rolling 4-week window: signals that are **confirming** (results already moving), signals that are **deepening** (the underlying luck getting stronger even as surface stats lag), and the honest misses where I was wrong.

The official accuracy clock starts at Week 10 (mid-June). Everything until then is the data pipeline doing its job.

### Signal Deepening: The Names Still in the "Wrong" Direction

The hardest concept to communicate in this tracker: **signals can deepen before results move**. The luck score getting stronger while the wOBA lags is *not* a failure mode — it's the model identifying players whose surface stats haven't caught up yet.

Three cases that fit this pattern right now:

**Sal Stewart — Buy Low**
Luck score: +0.214 (April 22) → **+0.439** (May 4). Up 0.225 in the window.
His wOBA is .366 vs xwOBA .417. The underlying contact is excellent — the balls aren't falling in. Nothing about his swing metrics has deteriorated. The signal is deepening. This is "still waiting for the BABIP tide to turn," not a miss.

**Evan Carter — Buy Low**
Luck score: +0.227 (April 22) → **+0.449** (May 4). Up 0.222 in the window.
Carter's wOBA sits at .279, xwOBA at .381. That's a 102-point gap. For reference, Aaron Judge's xwOBA gap was ~60 points when we called him a buy low — and his wOBA has already responded (+40 pts). Carter's gap is larger. The model says the disconnect is even more extreme than it looks.

**José Ramírez — Buy Low**
Luck score: +0.508 → **+0.496**. Essentially flat.
wOBA .339, down from .363 baseline. This one's frustrating to watch — Ramírez is a FP top-10 player hitting like a replacement-level hitter. But the xwOBA signal says the contact quality is still there. This is the hardest test of signal patience. Ramírez's career BABIP and contact history make the luck explanation more plausible here than almost anyone in the dataset. Still waiting.

This one is trending toward a genuine miss. I'm not calling it refuted yet — the luck signal technically remains active — but the 4-week window is pointing the wrong direction and the FIP movement is real. Bradish watchers: the "wait for the luck to normalize" thesis is under stress.

### New Buy: Trent Grisham (15% owned)

This is the name I want you to know this week.

**BABIP:** .145  
**xwOBA:** .395  
**wOBA:** .290  
**Luck score:** +0.577  

A .145 BABIP is historically extreme. At that level, roughly 85% of batted balls are outs regardless of quality. Career BABIP for Grisham is around .290. The expected regression to his true contact quality would produce a wOBA jump of 70-100 points.

He's 15% owned. In most leagues, he's available right now.

The model isn't projecting Grisham to be a star — xwOBA .395 is solid, not elite. But the gap between where he is and where the contact quality says he should be is one of the widest in the dataset. At 15% ownership, this is exactly the kind of lottery ticket the signal is designed to surface.

### Launch Angle Alert: Matt Chapman (Sell High, 71% owned)

The model flagged Chapman as Sell High in April. Here's the structural confirmation.

**Launch angle delta: −17.2°**  
Career average: **21.0°**  
Current 2026: **3.7°**

A 17-degree drop in launch angle is one of the largest in the dataset. That's not sample size noise — that's a mechanical change. Flat contact produces groundballs and weak fly balls instead of line drives and home runs.

The luck score is −0.364 and holding. wOBA .314 vs xwOBA .279 — even the contact quality model says the results look *better* than the underlying skill. This is a rare double-signal: the luck model says sell, and the launch angle model says the contact itself has degraded.

Chapman owners: the window to sell for value is now, before the counting stats start reflecting the flat swing path.
